Korean Kimbap

📖 History of Kimbap

Kimbap (also spelled gimbap) is a popular Korean dish made from cooked rice and other ingredients rolled in dried seaweed sheets. While it resembles Japanese sushi, Kimbap has its own unique history and flavors.

Origin: Kimbap originated during the Japanese colonial period (1910-1945) when Korean chefs adapted Japanese norimaki to local tastes. After Korea's liberation, Kimbap evolved into its own distinct dish with Korean flavors like sesame oil, pickled radish, and Korean spinach.

Cultural Significance: Kimbap is a beloved picnic food, lunchbox staple, and popular choice for outdoor activities. The name "kimbap" literally means "seaweed rice" in Korean.

Modern Variations: Today, you can find many creative versions including cheese kimbap, tuna kimbap, and even vegetarian options!

Ingredients:

  • 5 sheets of gim (seaweed paper), roasted slightly (sometimes called “nori” from Japanese)
  • 4 cups cooked rice
  • ½ pound beef skirt steak (or tenderloin, or ground beef)
  • 1 large carrot, cut into matchsticks (about 1½ cup)
  • 8 to 10 ounces spinach (1 small bunch), blanched, rinsed in cold water, and strained
  • 5 strips of yellow pickled radish (use pre-cut danmuji or cut into 8 inch long strips)
  • 3 eggs
  • 3 garlic cloves
  • 2 teaspoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon plus 1 teaspoon brown (or white) sugar
  • 1½ teaspoon kosher salt
  • 2½ tablespoons toasted sesame oil
  • vegetable oil

Instructions:

  1. Season in ½ teaspoon kosher salt and 2 teaspoons toasted sesame oil over top with a rice scoop the cooked rice with sesame oil and salt. Let it cool slightly.
  2. Combine the blanched spinach, 2 minced garlic cloves, ½ teaspoon kosher salt, and 2 teaspoons toasted sesame oil in a bowl.Mix well by hand and put it on a large platter with the sliced yellow pickled radish.
  3. Combine the carrot matchsticks with ¼ teaspoon kosher salt. Mix well and let it sweat for 5 to 10 minutes. Heat a pan and add a few drops vegetable oil.
  4. Squeeze out excess water from the carrot, then saute for about 1 minute. Put it on the platter next to the spinach.
  5. Trim the fat from the skirt steaks and slice into ¼ inch wide, 3 to 5 inch strips. Put the strips into a bowl.   6. Crack 3 eggs in a bowl and add ¼ teaspoon kosher salt. Beat it with fork and remove the stringy chalaza.
  7. Drizzle a few drops of oil on a heated 10 to 12 inch non-stick pan. Wipe off the excess with a paper towel so only a thin sheen of oil remains. Turn down the heat to low and pour the egg mixture into the pan. Spread it into a large circle so it fills the pan.
  8. When the bottom of the egg is cooked, flip it over with a spatula. Remove from the heat and let it cook slowly in the hot pan for about 5 minutes, with the ultimate goal of keeping the egg as yellow as possible, and not brown.
  9. Cut it into ½ inch wide strips. Put it next to the spinach on the platter.
